# Comparative Framework Map

## Purpose

This document compares the Law of Totality against nearby existing concepts.

The goal is not to pretend that every component is historically new.

The goal is to isolate the possible original compression:

> a general structural-error formula based on local closure, valid scope, actual use, and excluded critical dependency.

## Matrix

| Existing concept | What it already captures | What it does not usually formalize in this exact way | Relation to Law of Totality |
|---|---|---|---|
| Principle of Sufficient Reason | Nothing is without reason, ground, or explanation. | It does not usually become an operational audit of scope misuse. | Related to determinability and dependency. |
| Fallibilism | Beliefs and theories may be wrong; certainty is limited. | It often remains epistemological rather than operational. | Related to non-absolute closure. |
| Epistemic holism | Statements depend on broader theoretical networks. | It does not usually define an error condition by actual use exceeding valid scope. | Related to non-autonomous fragments. |
| Open systems theory | Systems interact with environments. | It is usually domain-specific and not always tied to model-use error. | Related to `x ≠ Ω` having external relation. |
| Closed-world assumption | What is not known may be treated as false inside some systems. | It does not by itself identify when that assumption becomes structurally invalid. | Related to false closure. |
| Model risk management | Models fail when misused, poorly specified, or used outside assumptions. | Usually finance/regulatory, not formulated as an omnia-disciplinary law. | Very close operationally. |
| Distribution shift | Model input distribution changes between training and deployment. | ML-specific instance of the broader structure. | Specific case of actual use exceeding valid scope. |
| Domain adaptation | Attempts to handle changed domains. | Usually technical ML method, not general structural-error law. | Corrective response to scope shift. |
| Model cards | Documents intended uses, limitations, and evaluation. | Documentation practice, not general error ontology. | Operationally compatible. |
| Datasheets for datasets | Documents data origin, motivation, composition, and use. | Dataset-specific. | Handles dependencies at data level. |
| Safety cases | Argues that a system is acceptably safe under assumptions. | Engineering-specific and often case-based. | Compatible with dependency-preserving validation. |
| Medical premature closure | Diagnosis stops too early. | Domain-specific; not generalized to all framework misuse. | Direct clinical manifestation of structural closure error. |
| Category error | A concept is applied in the wrong logical category. | Focuses on type/category, not necessarily dependency exclusion. | Related but narrower. |
| Bounded rationality | Agents decide under limited information and constraints. | Does not necessarily define false closure as the core error. | Explains finite-agent conditions. |
| Scientific idealization | Models simplify reality deliberately. | Often accepts simplification without formalizing misuse beyond scope. | Local closure is legitimate until exported improperly. |

## Preliminary conclusion

The strongest defensible position is:

```text
Many traditions contain partial fragments.
The Law of Totality attempts to compress those fragments into one operational structure:
structural error = local closure + use beyond valid scope + excluded critical dependency.
```

The weakest position is:

```text
Nobody has ever thought anything like this.
```

That claim should be avoided.

## Novelty test

A future literature search should ask:

> Is there an existing source that already formulates error as the conjunction of:
> local closure, scope overrun, actual use, and excluded critical dependency,
> under a general law of non-closure of proper manifestations?

If yes, the repository is a reformulation.

If no, the repository may contain a novel synthesis or formal compression.
