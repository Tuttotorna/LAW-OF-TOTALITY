# One-Page Claim

## Law of Totality

Structural error arises when a local theoretical closure is used as sufficient in a real situation that requires dependencies the theory has excluded.

---

## The core distinction

Local correctness is not structural validity.

A framework can be correct inside its own assumptions and invalid for the actual use made of it.

This happens when the framework is treated as sufficient while excluding a dependency that is critical for the real situation.

---

## Minimal structure

Structural error requires three conditions:

1. local closure;
2. use beyond valid scope;
3. excluded critical dependency.

In compact form:

> local closure + use beyond valid scope + excluded critical dependency = structural error

---

## Why this matters

Many failures are not caused by obvious falsehood.

They are caused by correct local structures used as if they were sufficient for a wider real field.

Examples:

- AI hallucination;
- unsafe engineering deployment;
- invalid medical generalization;
- economic forecast failure;
- legal answer outside jurisdiction;
- operational decision based on incomplete model.

The problem is not that the model is partial.

Every model is partial.

The problem is that the partial model is treated as total for the use at hand.

---

## Bridge claim

The Law of Totality does not reject theory.

It teaches a theory how to become real by forcing it to declare:

- what it closes;
- where it is valid;
- what use it serves;
- which dependencies that use requires;
- which required dependencies are excluded.

A theory is not structurally wrong because it is incomplete.

It becomes structurally wrong when its incompleteness is treated as sufficient completeness.

## Theory-reality friction

Theory closes.

Reality continues.

Structural error appears at the point where the closure of the theory is used as if it were the boundary of the real object.

---

## What the law does not say

It does not say that every model is false.

It does not say that abstraction is useless.

It does not say that partial knowledge must be rejected.

It says that every finite framework becomes structurally false when it is used beyond its valid scope while excluding a dependency required by the actual use.

---

## Final thesis

The fundamental error is not partiality.

The fundamental error is a fragment behaving as totality.
