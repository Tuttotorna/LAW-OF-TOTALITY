# Ω-Validity Calculus

## A formal calculus for preventing structural error in finite formulations

Version: v0.5.0

---

## 1. Purpose

The Law of Totality defines a structural form of error:

~~~text
Structural Error =
local closure
+ use beyond valid scope
+ exclusion of a critical dependency
~~~

The Ω-Validity Calculus is the next level.

It does not merely diagnose structural error after it appears.

It defines a validity architecture in which such an error cannot be authorized as a valid assertion.

The aim is not omniscience.

The aim is prevention of false closure.

---

## 2. Core Distinction

No finite framework can contain Ω.

But a finite framework can be prevented from behaving as if it were Ω.

Therefore:

~~~text
We cannot build a model that contains totality.
We can build a model that prevents every finite model from falsely acting as totality.
~~~

This is the central function of Ω-Validity Calculus.

---

## 3. Primitive Objects

Let:

~~~text
Ω = totality without outside
D = domain of the nameable, thinkable, modelable, formalizable, usable
x ∈ D = object, event, model, decision, answer, theory, system, or phenomenon
F = framework, model, theory, language, discipline, algorithm, or system
U = actual use of F on x or on an output a
a = assertion, output, answer, prediction, diagnosis, decision, formula, or claim
d = critical dependency, determinability-condition, or necessary constraint
~~~

---

## 4. Proper Manifestation

A proper manifestation is anything that manifests inside Ω but is not Ω.

~~~text
ProperΩ(x) ⇔ ManifestΩ(x) ∧ x ≠ Ω
~~~

For every proper manifestation:

~~~text
x has limit, relation, dependency, valid scope, or residual field.
~~~

Therefore:

~~~text
No x ≠ Ω can be absolutely closed.
~~~

---

## 5. Determinability Principle

A thing can manifest as x only if at least one condition makes it determinable as x.

~~~text
∀x ∈ D :
[ManifestΩ(x) ∧ x ≠ Ω]
⇒
∃d [DetCondΩ(d,x)]
~~~

In words:

~~~text
Nothing can manifest as something without conditions of determinability.
~~~

This is the ontological base.

---

## 6. Local Closure

Every framework F produces local closures.

~~~text
LocalClosureF(a)
~~~

means:

~~~text
F produces a as a locally closed output.
~~~

A local closure may be valid.

Local closure is not the error.

The error begins only when the local closure is used beyond its valid scope while excluding a critical dependency.

---

## 7. Valid Scope

Every output a generated by F must carry a valid scope.

~~~text
ValidScope(F,a)
~~~

means:

~~~text
the domain, conditions, assumptions, scale, data, and uses within which a can be treated as valid according to F.
~~~

A framework without declared scope is structurally dangerous.

It may be useful, but it is not yet structurally safe.

---

## 8. Actual Use

Validity depends not only on what F says.

It depends on how a is used.

~~~text
ActualUseU(F,a)
~~~

means:

~~~text
the concrete use made of output a produced by framework F.
~~~

A statement can be correct in F and invalid in U.

Therefore:

~~~text
CorrectF(a) does not imply ValidΩ(a,F,U).
~~~

---

## 9. Critical Dependency

A dependency d is critical when the use U requires it.

~~~text
CriticalDepΩ(d,a,U)
~~~

means:

~~~text
d is necessary for a to be validly used in U.
~~~

Examples:

~~~text
source
date
domain
scale
measurement
jurisdiction
population
initial condition
experimental verification
interpretive assumption
causal constraint
operational responsibility
safety condition
data provenance
temporal validity
~~~

---

## 10. Excluded Dependency

~~~text
ExcludedF(d)
~~~

means:

~~~text
F does not contain, verify, preserve, declare, or account for d.
~~~

A dependency may be excluded because it is:

~~~text
ignored
unknown
unmeasured
undeclared
unavailable
outside scale
outside domain
outside model
assumed irrelevant
treated as closed
~~~

## 11. Structural Error Formula

~~~text
ErrΩ(a,F,U) ⇔
LocalClosureF(a)
∧ ActualUseU(F,a) exceeds ValidScope(F,a)
∧ ∃d [CriticalDepΩ(d,a,U) ∧ ExcludedF(d)]
~~~

In words:

~~~text
Structural error occurs when a local closure is used beyond its valid scope while excluding a critical dependency.
~~~

---

## 12. Structural Validity Formula

To prevent structural error, every valid assertion must satisfy:

~~~text
ValidΩ(a,F,U) ⇔
CorrectF(a)
∧ ActualUseU(F,a) ⊆ ValidScope(F,a)
∧ ∀d [CriticalDepΩ(d,a,U) ⇒ ¬ExcludedF(d)]
~~~

In words:

An assertion is structurally valid only if:

~~~text
1. it is correct within F;
2. its actual use remains inside its valid scope;
3. every critical dependency required by the use is preserved, declared, or not excluded.
~~~

---

## 13. Fundamental Difference

Classical correctness:

~~~text
CorrectF(a)
~~~

Structural validity:

~~~text
ValidΩ(a,F,U)
~~~

They are not equivalent.

A statement may be locally correct and structurally invalid.

This is the core distinction:

~~~text
Correct is not stable.
Local truth is not structural validity.
~~~

---

## 14. Assertion Rule

A system S governed by Ω-Validity Calculus may assert a only if:

~~~text
AssertS(a,F,U) ⇒ ValidΩ(a,F,U)
~~~

This is the preventive directive.

No output may be asserted as structurally valid without passing through scope and dependency control.

---

## 15. Structural Error Prevention Theorem

If every assertion in S requires ValidΩ(a,F,U), then S cannot authorize ErrΩ(a,F,U).

~~~text
[AssertS(a,F,U) ⇒ ValidΩ(a,F,U)]
⇒
[AssertS(a,F,U) ⇒ ¬ErrΩ(a,F,U)]
~~~

In words:

~~~text
A system that authorizes outputs only through structural validity cannot authorize structural error.
~~~

This does not make the system omniscient.

It makes false closure structurally impossible within the defined error class.

## 16. Output States

An Ω-valid system should not produce only true/false outputs.

It should produce structural states.

### 16.1 Structurally Valid

~~~text
CorrectF(a)
ActualUseU(F,a) ⊆ ValidScope(F,a)
No critical dependency is excluded.
~~~

### 16.2 Locally Correct, Not Structurally Valid

~~~text
CorrectF(a)
but ActualUseU(F,a) exceeds ValidScope(F,a)
or a critical dependency is missing.
~~~

### 16.3 Open / Dependency Missing

~~~text
At least one critical dependency required by U is unavailable or excluded.
~~~

### 16.4 Scope Violation

~~~text
The intended use exceeds the declared valid scope of F.
~~~

### 16.5 Structural Error Blocked

~~~text
LocalClosureF(a)
+ use beyond valid scope
+ excluded critical dependency
has been detected before assertion.
~~~

~~~text

~~~

The structural error is:

~~~text
AI hallucination =
local linguistic closure
+ factual use beyond valid scope
+ excluded critical dependency
~~~

Examples of excluded dependencies:

~~~text
source verification
current date
jurisdiction
domain-specific evidence
real-world measurement
document access
causal confirmation
expert validation
~~~

An AI governed by Ω-Validity Calculus must not merely correct hallucination after output.

If a critical dependency is missing, the AI cannot assert closure.

It must return:

~~~text
open state
scope limitation
dependency missing
verification required
~~~

## 18. Theory of Everything Relation

A physical Theory of Everything may attempt to close a physical domain.

But it remains a framework F.

Therefore:

~~~text
A physical Theory of Everything may be a maximal physical closure.
It cannot be Ω.
~~~

The Law of Totality and Ω-Validity Calculus do not compete with a physical Theory of Everything.

They govern the condition under which any theory, including a physical Theory of Everything, may be structurally valid.

A theory of everything that treats itself as Ω commits structural error.

A theory of everything that declares its scope and preserves its dependencies remains locally valid and structurally open.
